The first time I played Elden Ring, I died to the Tree Sentinel outside the Church of Elleh approximately thirty times before walking around him. A year later, returning for a second playthrough, I killed him on the second try.

This is the whole game in miniature. It is a system that teaches you by refusing to explain itself^(FromSoftware's design philosophy is essentially: the game contains all the information you need; your job is to learn to read it.). You learn by dying, by exploring, by paying attention to enemy telegraphing and level geometry and the implications of item descriptions.

What I noticed on the second pass: the world is radically more generous than it first appears. There are messages from other players, yes, but there is also a kind of architectural generosity in how the levels are designed. Paths loop back on themselves. Shortcuts reveal themselves as rewards for exploration. The verticality of the zones — the way you can see something far below or far above and eventually find a route to it — creates a sense of the world as a coherent space rather than a series of arenas.

The lore remains maximally obscure. I have read probably forty hours of wiki articles about the story and I still do not feel like I understand it, which I mean as a compliment.
