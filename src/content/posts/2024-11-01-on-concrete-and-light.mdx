There is a particular quality of light that only exists on west-facing concrete walls at four in the afternoon. It is the kind of light that makes you understand why people built entire philosophies around material honesty^(The idea that a building's materials should be left exposed and unfinished — concrete as concrete, brick as brick — rather than covered over with plaster or paint.).

I have spent more time than I can account for standing in front of buildings most people consider ugly. The parking garage downtown. The brutalist library on campus. The housing towers that everyone wants torn down. There is something in the repetition of their poured forms that reads, to me, as deeply earnest. They are not trying to be anything other than what they are.

This is not a defense of brutalism as such. It is more of a note about attention.

When you start looking at concrete closely — really looking, not just registering that a wall is there — you begin to notice the wood-grain texture left by the formwork, the slight variations in color where different pours met, the small pebbles exposed at the surface. You begin to see evidence of all the decisions that were made and all the labor that went into producing something that most people walk past without a second glance.

I think this is what photography teaches, if it teaches anything: that most of the visible world is unobserved. That there is more in front of you than you are currently seeing. That the act of framing is also the act of paying attention^(The camera becomes a permission slip to stare at things without social awkwardness. You are allowed to look at the parking garage for ten minutes if you have a camera.).

The concrete wall at four o'clock is just a wall. Until it isn't.
