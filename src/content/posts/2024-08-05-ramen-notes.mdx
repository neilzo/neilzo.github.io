The best bowl of ramen I have ever eaten was in a twelve-seat shop in a neighborhood I found by accident, on a street that didn't appear on any of the maps I'd downloaded. The second-best was at a train station. These two facts contain the entire epistemology of ramen^(Which is: the system that predicts quality is always incomplete. The Michelin star can tell you something but not the thing that matters most.).

A few things I have learned:

**On broth:** The quality of a tonkotsu broth is not about richness exactly. It is about depth — whether the flavor continues to develop as you eat or whether it settles into a single note. The best broths are conversations.

**On noodles:** Firmness on order ("katame") is almost always correct. The noodle will continue cooking in the bowl.

**On timing:** Eat fast. The ramen is engineered for the first four minutes. After that you are eating a different dish, which is also fine but is not the intended experience.

**On atmosphere:** The counter seat facing the kitchen is always preferable. You want to watch the work.
